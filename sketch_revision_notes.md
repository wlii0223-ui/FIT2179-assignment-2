# Sketch Revision Notes

These notes are based on the current hand-drawn sketch. The existing sketch has a usable structure, but it should be strengthened if the goal is HD.

## Current Sketch: What Works

- The topic is clear: natural disasters across Australia.
- The opening research question is strong:
  - Where have DRFA disaster activations concentrated since 2006?
  - Which communities are more exposed after accounting for population?
- The sketch already has several useful chart ideas:
  - Australia map
  - Hazard type bar chart
  - Regional comparison chart
  - Time series chart
  - Multi-line hazard trend chart
  - Per-capita exposure dot plot
  - Top LGA comparison
- The story roughly moves from national overview to hazard profile, time change and community exposure.

## Main Problems to Fix

### 1. The First Section Needs to Look More Important

The map is currently too plain. For the final sketch, make the first map the visual anchor of the page.

Change it to:

- A large Australia map occupying most of the first section.
- Use points or shaded areas to show disaster records / activations.
- Add a legend for hazard type colours.
- Add 3 annotation callouts:
  - Flood concentration on the east coast.
  - Bushfire exposure around Victoria / NSW / SA.
  - Cyclone exposure in northern Australia.

This makes the first section immediately look like a serious data visualisation, not just a placeholder map.

### 2. Add Clear Section Titles and Takeaways

Your section titles are currently questions, which is good, but each section also needs a short answer.

Recommended format:

- Section title: question.
- Small bold takeaway: answer.
- Then charts.

Example:

**Where are disaster activations concentrated?**  
Flood and rainfall activations dominate the east coast, while cyclone exposure is concentrated in the north.

### 3. The Second Page Is Too Empty

Section 4 currently has only two chart ideas and a lot of blank space. For HD, use this page to show the strongest "so what?" part: repeated exposure and human consequences.

Add:

- AIHW statistic cards.
- Age / sex impact chart.
- A historical impact bubble chart.
- Metadata footer.

### 4. Make the 10+ Charts Obvious

The marker needs to see chart variety quickly. Label each chart with a small chart number in the sketch.

Use:

1. National disaster map
2. Disaster type ranking
3. Historical top impact events
4. DRFA yearly trend
5. Stacked area by hazard type
6. State comparison
7. Hazard profile by region
8. Per-capita exposure dot plot
9. Top LGAs by activation count
10. State x hazard heatmap
11. AIHW impact stat cards
12. Age / sex impact chart
13. Historical impact bubble chart

You do not need all 13 in the final if the page becomes crowded, but the sketch should show strong variety.

## What to Redraw

Do not redraw everything from scratch as a polished artwork. Keep it hand-drawn, but make these improvements:

- Draw stronger section boundaries.
- Number each chart.
- Make the first map much larger.
- Fill the second page.
- Add legends and interaction controls.
- Add 3-5 short annotations.
- Replace the parallel-lines chart with a clearer top-LGA horizontal bar chart unless you have a very specific reason to use parallel coordinates.

## Best Version of the Story

Use this final narrative order:

1. **Where disasters hit:** national spatial footprint.
2. **What dominates:** flood, bushfire, storm and cyclone profiles.
3. **How it changes:** recovery activations over time.
4. **Who is repeatedly exposed:** LGAs and states with recurring activations.
5. **Why it matters:** human injury and death impact.

This is stronger than the current sketch because it ends with consequence, not just another exposure chart.
