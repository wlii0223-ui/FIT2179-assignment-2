1. National footprint

Natural disasters are concentrated where people, climate and exposure meet.

Historical records show recurring flood, bushfire, storm, cyclone and heat events across Australia. The map starts with location and impact before the story shifts to recent recovery activations.

2. Modern recovery burden

Since 2006, flood and rainfall have dominated formal recovery activations.

The DRFA activation dataset records where disaster recovery assistance was activated. It does not measure every disaster directly, but it gives a strong view of repeated recovery demand.

3. Different places, different hazards

Every state has a different recovery profile.

Flood and rainfall appear across the country, while bushfire, storm and cyclone activations create different regional signatures. This section is designed for comparison rather than exploration.

State abbreviations: ACT = Australian Capital Territory; NSW = New South Wales; NT = Northern Territory; QLD = Queensland; SA = South Australia; TAS = Tasmania; VIC = Victoria; WA = Western Australia.

4. Repeated exposure

Some local government areas appear again and again.

Repeated activation is not a blame measure. It indicates communities where disaster recovery need has recurred, often because hazard exposure, settlement patterns and geography overlap.

5. Human consequences

The human cost is not only the spectacular disaster event.

AIHW injury reporting shows that forces of nature continue to cause hospitalisations and deaths. Historical impact records provide context for high-impact events, while recent health data keeps the final section focused on people.
