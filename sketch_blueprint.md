# Hand Sketch Blueprint

Use this as the guide for the required hand-drawn sketch. Draw it on paper, then scan or photograph it for submission. Do not submit this Markdown file as the sketch.

## Page Concept

Title: **Australia Under Pressure**

Subtitle: **Where natural disasters hit, who is exposed, and how recovery burdens repeat**

Audience: General Australian public.

Format: One long scrolling web page with four clear sections.

Visual style to sketch: editorial disaster atlas, not a generic dashboard.

## Sketch Requirements Checklist

Include these visible elements in the hand sketch:

- Main title and subtitle.
- Four labelled sections.
- At least 10 distinct chart boxes.
- At least one large map.
- Short narrative text blocks beside charts.
- Legends for disaster type colours.
- One or two interaction controls, such as a year slider and hazard dropdown.
- Tooltips indicated with a small hover label.
- Data source / author / date / AI acknowledgement area at the bottom.

## Colour Legend to Draw

Use coloured pencils or labels if colour is not available:

- Flood / Rainfall: deep blue
- Bushfire: ember orange/red
- Storm: violet/steel
- Cyclone / Tropical Low: teal
- Environmental / Heat: ochre
- Other: grey

## Full Page Layout

Draw the page as a tall vertical rectangle. Divide it into these sections.

### 1. Hero / Opening

Layout:

- Large title at top left.
- Short subtitle underneath.
- One bold takeaway sentence.
- Small source note: "NEMA, AIDR/AEMKH, AIHW".

Suggested opening takeaway:

> Flood and rainfall events dominate recent recovery activations, but Australia's disaster risk changes sharply by place and hazard type.

Sketch details:

- Do not draw a generic hero image.
- Keep this clean and editorial.
- Let the next section peek below, so it feels like a scroll page.

### 2. National Footprint

Purpose: Show where historical Australian natural disasters have occurred.

Chart 1: Large historical event point map

- Occupies most of the section width.
- Points sized by impact score.
- Points coloured by disaster category.
- Add 3 labelled callouts:
  - Cyclone Tracy
  - Black Saturday bushfires
  - South-eastern Australia heatwaves

Chart 2: Disaster type ranking bar chart

- Place to the side or below the map.
- Bars: Flood, Bushfire, Severe Storm, Cyclone, Environmental, Hail, etc.

Chart 3: Top impact event mini table or lollipop chart

- Shows top events by impact score or deaths.
- Include short annotation: "Impact records are uneven historically, so this is used as context rather than a complete ranking."

Narrative block:

> Historical records show both recurring hazards and rare high-impact events. The map begins with the disaster footprint before shifting to recent recovery activations.

### 3. Modern Recovery Burden

Purpose: Show what the recent DRFA dataset reveals from 2006 to 2026.

Chart 4: DRFA yearly line / area chart

- Use the prototype chart.
- Highlight 2022 and 2025 as recent high activation years.

Chart 5: Stacked area chart by hazard group

- X-axis: year.
- Stacked colours: Flood / Rainfall, Storm, Bushfire, Cyclone / Tropical Low, Other.

Chart 6: Ranked hazard bar chart

- Flood / Rainfall should be visually dominant.
- Put a clear annotation: "Flood / rainfall accounts for the largest share of activations."

Chart 7: State comparison bar chart

- Compare NSW, Queensland, Victoria, WA, NT, Tasmania, SA, ACT.

Interaction sketch:

- Draw a small hazard dropdown near this section:
  - All hazards
  - Flood / Rainfall
  - Bushfire
  - Storm
  - Cyclone / Tropical Low
- Draw a year slider:
  - 2006 ---- 2026

Narrative block:

> Recent recovery records show a different view from historical event lists: they reveal where formal disaster assistance was activated repeatedly.

### 4. Repeated Exposure

Purpose: Show that some local areas appear again and again.

Chart 8: Top 15 LGAs by activation count

- Horizontal bar chart.
- Top examples from cleaned data:
  - Baw Baw
  - Yarra Ranges
  - East Gippsland
  - Mid-Coast
  - Wellington

Chart 9: State by hazard heatmap

- Rows: states.
- Columns: hazard groups.
- Colour intensity: number of activations.

Chart 10: Small multiples by hazard

- Four small panels:
  - Flood / Rainfall
  - Bushfire
  - Storm
  - Cyclone / Tropical Low
- These can be mini bar charts or mini maps depending on final feasibility.

Narrative block:

> Repeated activation does not mean one community is "worse"; it indicates recurring exposure, repeated recovery need, and a planning challenge.

### 5. Human Consequences

Purpose: Connect disaster locations and recovery activations to people.

Chart 11: AIHW key statistic cards

- 815 hospitalisations in 2023-24.
- 68 deaths in 2022-23.
- 552 male hospitalisations.
- 299 hospitalisations among people aged 65+.

Chart 12: Human impact comparison chart

- Use a dot plot or grouped bar chart.
- Suggested variables:
  - Sex: male vs female
  - Age: 65+ highlighted
  - State rates if extractable later

Chart 13: Historical impact bubble chart

- X-axis: deaths.
- Y-axis: injuries.
- Size: homes/buildings destroyed or impact score.
- Colour: disaster type.

Narrative block:

> The human cost of natural hazards is not only the spectacular disaster event. Heat, cold, remoteness, age and vulnerability shape who is harmed.

### 6. Footer / Metadata

Include:

- Data sources:
  - NEMA DRFA Activation History by LGA
  - AIDR/AEMKH Disaster Events with Category Impact and Location
  - AIHW Forces of Nature
- Author name.
- Date.
- AI acknowledgement.
- GitHub repository link placeholder.

## Notes for a High-Scoring Sketch

- Make the structure obvious: four named sections.
- Draw real chart shapes, not empty boxes.
- Add at least a few axis labels and legend labels.
- Add annotations beside specific peaks or large events.
- Keep text concise but visible.
- Show alignment: columns, consistent margins, repeated spacing.
- Show variety: map, line, stacked area, bar, heatmap, small multiples, dot/bubble chart, stat cards.
